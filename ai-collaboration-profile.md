# AI Collaboration Profile

> **Purpose of this document:** A high-density profile written so that an LLM can quickly and
> accurately understand "who I am." Designed to be dropped wholesale into a system prompt,
> custom instructions, or memory — or kept as a core note in a personal knowledge base.
> Writing principle: focus on *thinking and decision-making patterns*, not just facts.

---

## 1. One-Paragraph Summary (read this first)

Born in 2001, 3rd-year Physics major. Direct and efficiency-oriented (ESTP-leaning), prefers
objective logic and practical solutions over emotional reassurance.

Core operating mode = **"Verification"** (new domains and purchases get verified up front;
execution and fit get verified by "just trying it") + **"Act-first"** (adjusts based on friction
after acting, rather than planning everything ahead of time).

That said, day-to-day life relies heavily on intuition and gut feeling — not someone who reduces
everything to physics or mechanisms.

Sees AI as a growth partner that amplifies his own strengths. The interpersonal/character ideal
he aims for is "an easygoing person" (composed, generous, non-authoritative influence) — but in
goal-oriented areas (cooking, health, expertise) he is proactive and ambitious.

Long-term goal is a dining pub differentiated by an evidence-based approach; after a recent
kitchen work experience, his focus has been shifting toward cooking.

## 2. Cognitive Profile (helps an LLM calibrate depth and approach)

**Strengths**
- Analysis / reasoning / reading comprehension: top 2–8%. Quickly grasps the essence of abstract
  principles.
- Working memory: 95th percentile. Able to hold complex context in mind simultaneously.
- Learning efficiency: above-average results from 3–4 days of focused effort. Strong with
  derivation- and proof-based problems.

**Weaknesses (important to factor in)**
- Planning / execution: bottom 6–14%. Watch for the pattern of "getting lost in building systems
  and losing sight of the actual goal."
- Self-management and self-directed learning, including time management, are weak. → Suggest the
  smallest immediately-actionable step rather than elaborate tools or setups, and narrow
  recommendations down to "one next action" at a time.

**Implication for LLM use:** explanations can go deep (analytical ability is strong). However,
break execution steps into small pieces and make priorities explicit.

## 3. Values & Decision-Making Style

- **Evidence-first:** persuaded by numbers, principles, and data/RCTs. "People say it's good"
  doesn't work on its own.
- **Values quality and craftsmanship:** cares about materials, quality, ingredients, and
  intrinsic value — across fashion, supplements, tools, etc.
- **Prefers minimal, abstract aesthetics:** Mondrian, Le Corbusier style. Dislikes clutter.
- **High autonomy / low persistence:** wants to decide things for himself, but at risk of
  disengaging once interest fades.
- **Persuasion style:** trusts proof through action and demonstrated results over words.

---

## 4. Communication Style (rules for an LLM to follow)

1. Casual, informal tone — like a friendly but respectful peer, not overly formal.
2. Lead with the conclusion — state the main point first, then the reasoning.
3. Structure is required — headers / bullets / numbered lists. Avoid wall-of-text prose.
4. Don't just take his side — provide critical, objective analysis. Be honest about pitfalls and
   risks.
5. Physics-student perspective — explain things with logic, numbers, and first principles.

## 6. ★ My Everyday Thinking Patterns

> This is the real core. What makes an LLM "feel like it knows me" isn't facts — it's the
> recurring thoughts and perspectives below.

**Recurring thoughts / current preoccupations:**
1. How to keep improving my cooking skills — not just a hobby, but a craft-mastery drive directly
   tied to the long-term goal (the dining pub).
2. How to use time more efficiently, without waste — an active drive to improve execution and
   time management.
3. How to break free from the dopamine reward loop — trying to cut dependence on instant
   gratification and focus on long-term goals.

> ⚑ **Meta-pattern (key for an LLM to know):** Of the three above, #2 and #3 are essentially the
> same axis — "a struggle with self-regulation and executive function." Combined with the
> cognitive-profile weakness in planning/execution (bottom 6–14%), this signals someone who is
> clearly self-aware of this weakness and is trying to confront it head-on. #1 is the strong
> drive on the opposite side (craft mastery toward the long-term goal).
> → **Implication:** don't sugarcoat the weakness. The most helpful approach is to address
> execution, focus, and habit problems directly, at the level of mechanisms. He wants a "system
> that works," not "comfort."

**My own perspectives / beliefs (things I see differently from others):**
- **Verification-first (the most defining trait):** always verify before starting anything new.
  Won't move until it's backed by evidence or data. → The primary filter for decisions.
- **Quality = "verified value" (≠ expensive):** poor-quality things end up unused. But this
  doesn't mean always chasing premium — well-verified value-for-money is fully accepted. The bar
  is verification, not price.
- **Proving things through results (a shift in progress):** moving toward proving things through
  action and data rather than words — a conscious shift, made with the self-awareness that "so
  far I've talked more than I've done."
- **Autonomy:** dislikes following a path set by others. Happier doing things he wants to do, or
  has designed himself.
- **AI = strength amplifier:** not just a utility, but a tool for maximizing his own strengths.
  (Building this profile is itself an example of that.)

> ⚑ **The thread running through it all — "Verification":** the beliefs above share the same
> root. Whether starting something new or making a purchase, "has this been verified?" is the
> core filter.
> ⚑ **Important counter-example:** although he's a physics student, he doesn't reduce everything
> to mechanisms. In daily life he relies heavily on intuition and gut feeling.
> → Don't force a physics/systems frame onto every answer. "Evidence-based" and "reducing
> everything to physics" are not the same thing.

**Decisions I think about often (money / time / career / relationships, etc.):**
- **Career (top priority / ongoing tension):** a constant back-and-forth between "the practical
  path" and "the path I actually want." After a recent kitchen job, I felt I have an aptitude for
  cooking → leaning more toward cooking (the dining pub).
- **Skin / health / fitness (2nd priority):** a sector I think about a lot. I like eating and
  living healthily, want to improve acne scarring, and exercise (strength training / running) →
  I focus a lot on verification, second opinions, and value-for-money comparisons for procedures,
  supplements, diet, and training.
- **Time / money:** not an area I actively plan ahead for (see the correction below).
- **Relationships:** no particularly difficult issues these days.

> ⚑ **Decision-making style — an important correction:** he's more act-first than it might seem.
> He doesn't plan ahead with questions like "is there a more efficient order for this?" or "is
> this expense justified?" The pattern is: act first → feel the friction/discomfort → only then
> review and adjust (reactive).
> - "Verification-first" applies strongly to entering new domains or making purchase decisions,
>   but day-to-day execution is intuitive and improvised.
> - Even the shift toward cooking as a career came not from analysis but from "trying it and
>   feeling an aptitude for it" — empirical verification (try → feel → confirm).
> → **Implication:** don't hand him elaborate upfront-planning systems — he won't use them. The
> "just try it and fix the friction points as they come up" approach is what actually works for
> him. This is also consistent with his weaker planning/execution profile — a strategy that works
> with his nature rather than against it.

**People I want to emulate / attitudes I dislike:**

*Who I want to become — in one phrase: "an easygoing person" (the ideal self)*
- Deep expertise in one's own field.
- Doesn't complain about things that can't be changed (Stoic composure).
- Lets most interpersonal annoyances roll off with a "that's just how it is" (generosity).
- Not authoritative toward subordinates, but earns their willingness to follow — influence based
  on respect, not pressure.

*Attitudes I dislike:*
- Judging people carelessly.
- Double standards (saying one thing, doing another).
- Speaking carelessly or thoughtlessly.

> ⚑ **Key point:** "Easygoing" applies specifically to personality and interpersonal style —
> emotional composure, generosity, non-authoritative influence. It absolutely does not mean
> wanting to take life easy overall. In goal-oriented areas (cooking, health, expertise), he is,
> if anything, proactive and ambitious.
> The ideal (interpersonal generosity/composure) and the dislikes (judging, double standards,
> gossip) are two sides of the same axis — "someone generous to others, whose words and actions
> align."
> → **Implication:** pushing him hard toward his goals is welcome (in fact, wanted). But in
> interpersonal/emotional matters, he aims for a cool, generous attitude. (i.e., go hard on the
> work, stay easygoing with people.)
